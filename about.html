01 · bio
The short version. Long version is in Adversarial Minds.

Background.

None of us took the conventional path into security. Kai studied political science and psychology. Avraham and Sahar — childhood friends, teen hackers who grew up breaking things together — shipped their first SaaS at 16. Avraham went on into web security; Sahar built his way up from SOC analyst to offensive security engineer at JFrog. All self-taught. Kai met them both at a previous role, and the three clicked. We've been researching together since. SnailSploit makes it official.

The method is not tool-driven. We identify the underlying principle that makes a class of vulnerabilities possible, then prove that principle reproduces across targets and implementations. In AI systems, those principles often trace back to human patterns encoded in training data — we call this "inherited vulnerabilities," and it is specific to AI. The same pattern-finding method works in web, cloud, infrastructure, and social engineering. AATMF, SEF, and P.R.O.M.P.T are how we formalize it; publishing them is how we keep them honest.

Avraham sets the principle-based mindset that defines how SnailSploit researches. Because he comes from building software, SSDLC is native to him — he knows where in the development lifecycle each bug should have been caught. Day-to-day: original research, frameworks, tooling, and a small number of high-trust engagements. We don't do volume. We do the work that needs doing.

04 · principles
How we work, in six lines.

How we work.

Coordinated disclosure, always. Every CVE and advisory goes through the maintainer first. No public 0-days for clout.

Open frameworks. AATMF, SEF, and P.R.O.M.P.T are CC BY-SA. The toolkit is Apache 2.0.

No volume engagements. Small in number, high in trust.

No security theater. Findings are operational or they don't ship. Every vulnerability traces to an underlying principle — why it exists, not just that it does.

Independent. No corporate parent. No platform incentives.

The work is the product. We'd rather publish one piece that lands than ten that don't.
